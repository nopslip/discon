Jason Jarvis

Talk Abstract: Though super computers have been built for over 50 years, it took the coordinated processing and socioeconomic phenomenon of bitcoin and the blockchain PoW algorithm to truly give us this next generation of commoditized data processing.

Bio: Jason has some early stories about performance computing builds. A favorite is about his first notable GPU build in ~1997 for a University professor that was running NASTRAN, a finite element analysis program developed by NASA. In Jason's words "We built an Intel Pentium II MMX ( with L1 cache of 16kb), megabytes of ram and hard disk space, and suspiciously a Matrox Millenium video card. My coworkers were thrilled, the sales guy made a fortune(I am sure) but we burned it in playing Doom. So, I get the job of delivering the machine. It was great and not really what I expected. He's there eagerly awaiting the delivery of his new computer. I set it up get him logged in. He then opens a cmd prompt, inserts a floppy disk and runs a simple CAD simulation command. We sit there and literally in the command window is line data, 1 Mississippi.. line data 1 Mississippi, ..line data 1 Mississippi, the professor looks back at me, smiles, and says "man that's fast". I just gave him the nod said I am headed out, and left while he continued to watch line data, 1 Mississippi.. line data 1 Mississippi, ..line data 1 Mississippi... sweet"

Many years and projects later, Jason remembers the conversation when he first heard of "bitcoin mining" with a colleague of his. Colleague said "I have been mining bitcoin with video hardware kinda like playing a game". Jason thought hmm, really?, and then immediately rolled up his sleeves to provide his friend with a GPU accelerated Windows Hyper-V Workstation. Friend was impressed, and Jason's appetite for bitcoin mining began.

By this point in time Jason had already been heating his house by running FEM simulation hardware and encryption services and regularly had a utility bill of >2.5X his neighborhood average. Jason then started mixing in bitcoin mining.

Blockchain and the emergence of cryptocurrencies has helped coordinated performance computing in so many ways. OpenCL and NVidia grid computing's successes can directly correlate to the emergence of crypto-mining. Now an ASIC the size of a thumb drive can SHA256 work at gigahash rates!

Alex Stokes

Talk Abstract: Blockchains coordinate human behavior at unprecedented scales; commensurately, we now face a governance challenge unlike any other. This talk presents a solution to the decentralized governance of blockchain protocols inspired by futarchy. Under this paradigm, a system-wide metric is used to determine the efficacy of proposals predicted to improve the system's utility via the action of prediction markets. By choosing a metric tied to the behavior of the blockchain's validators, we find an incentive-compatible governance mechanism that avoids the failings of a variety of other solutions in the ecosystem. We conclude by surveying these alternatives and show how this system improves upon their shortcomings through the lens of cryptoeconomics.

Bio: Alex Stokes (@ralexstokes) is a cryptoeconomist who has been studying blockchains since 2011 while at Princeton University. He thinks that governance is the biggest challenge blocking the widespread adoption of these systems and spends his time leading the discourse on how we can best collectively use the "decentralized commons". Outside of his work on governance, Alex is an active open source contributor to cryptocurrency projects like Ethereum's Casper proof-of-stake protocol. He believes that token markets and AI ethics have more in common than it may at first appear ;)

Luke Stokes

Talk Abstract: Beginning in March of 2017, we've seen Bitcoin dominance drop from above 80% down to as low as the mid 30s. The reasons for this are hotly debated, but it's undeniable space has been made for new approaches including DPOS, Delegated Proof of Stake, which powers the most active blockchains on the planet including BitShares, STEEM, and more recently, EOS. This cooperation over competition model for securing blockchains goes beyond protocol consensus to include on-chain governance. This new environment allows not only for DACs, Distributed Autonomous Communities, but for a new token-driven economy where incentives between token holders, block producers, users, and workers are in full alignment

In this talk, we'll explore DPOS, DACs, and on-chain governance. We'll see how the promise of blockchain disruption is being realized through enterprise-ready solutions for real-world applications. Examples will include the BitShares DEX, Steemit, DTube, DLive, Utopian.io, and more.
